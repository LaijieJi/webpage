## Back to the Path

After spending so much time in the world of the Witcher, through the books, and the late nights playing the games, I did not think Sapkowski could still surprise me with Geralt. *Crossroads of Ravens* proved me wrong. Instead of the weary, sharp-tongued witcher I already knew, this book hands you a much younger Geralt, barely out of Kaer Morhen, still figuring out what kind of man, or monster, the world expects him to be.

It is a story about beginnings, and that is exactly what makes it so interesting.

## The first steps of a witcher

What I enjoyed the most was watching Geralt at the very start of his Path. He is fast and deadly, trained for a single purpose, and yet he is also naive, unsure, and far more human than the legend he would later become. Sapkowski takes his time here, showing the small contracts, the mistakes, the doubts, and the slow realisation of how the world actually treats someone like him.

Seeing the origin of so many things I had taken for granted in the later stories gave the whole saga a new weight. You understand that the cold, distant Geralt was not born that way. He was shaped, slowly and painfully, by everything that happens on this road.

## Evil, and the refusal to choose

The part that stayed with me the most was watching the seed of Geralt's philosophy being planted. Faced again and again with situations where there is no clean answer, where every path leads to someone getting hurt, he begins to wrestle with the idea of the lesser evil. People want him to pick a side, to decide which evil is more acceptable, and he simply refuses.

> If I have to choose between one evil and another, I would rather not choose at all.

It is easy to read that as stubbornness, but the more I thought about it, the more it felt like the only honest position. Calling something the lesser evil is still calling it evil, and pretending otherwise is how people make peace with terrible things. Watching a young Geralt arrive at this on his own, before he even has the words for it, was the emotional core of the book for me.

## Who the real monsters are

The other thread that hit hard was the discrimination the witchers live with. They are mutants, created to protect people from the creatures that hunt them in the dark, and their reward is to be feared, insulted, and treated as freaks. They are called monsters to their faces by the very people they bleed for.

And yet, again and again, the book quietly shows you who the real monsters are. The humans, with their fear, their greed, and their cruelty, often do far worse than anything Geralt is paid to hunt. The creatures act on instinct; people choose. That contrast, monsters by birth against monsters by choice, is where Sapkowski has always been at his best.

## Final thoughts

*Crossroads of Ravens* is not a grand, world-shaking adventure, and I think that is the point. It is a quiet, sharp look at how a legend begins, and at the small moral choices that slowly turn a young man into the witcher we know. If you already love this world, it adds a layer you did not know you were missing. And if monsters, the human kind especially, are something you like to think about, give it a chance. You will not look at Geralt the same way again.
