## The story

A generation after the bombs fell, the survivors of Moscow live crammed into the tunnels of the metro, each station its own small, wary nation. Artyom cannot accept that this is all that remains of the world, and keeps climbing to the poisoned surface with a radio, hunting for any signal that other people are still alive out there. *Metro 2035* is the final book of Dmitry Glukhovsky's trilogy.

## The end of the line

*Metro 2035* was the last stop in a journey that started years ago, when I picked up some of the first science fiction I ever read. The Metro books, along with everything in that same Russian, post-apocalyptic, STALKER kind of mood, were the stories that pulled me into the genre in the first place. So coming to the final novel of Glukhovsky's saga felt like saying goodbye to something that had quietly shaped the way I read. And to my surprise, it might just be the best of them all.

## Coming back to Artyom

A big part of that is Artyom. *Metro 2034* had stepped away from him to follow Hunter, and as much as I appreciated that detour, I had missed him. Returning to his point of view, to his stubbornness and his impossible hope, felt like coming home. Homer was a wonderful presence too, the old chronicler still trying to turn all of this misery into a story worth remembering. Together they carry the weight of the book without ever making it feel heavy.

## The world above

What shook me the most was the slow, terrible revelation that there were people living outside the Metro network all along. The world above was never as empty as everyone had been told.

The chapter that stayed with me is the one where Artyom tries to tell the people of the Metro the truth: that there is a world out there, that they could climb back up, that they could try to live something close to a normal life again. And the answer he gets is not anger, or fear, or even disbelief. It is indifference. They simply do not care.

> They had spent so long in the dark that the light became the thing they feared most.

That detail broke me a little. These people had lived underground for so long, had been hurt so deeply, that hope itself had become unbearable to them. It was easier to stay in the dark they knew than to risk the daylight they had already given up on. And it is hard not to see how real that is. You can find the same exhaustion in people living through the wars happening right now, people so worn down that the idea of a different life slowly stops feeling possible.

## Final thoughts

Artyom gets something like a happy ending, and he earns it. But the quiet tragedy of *Metro 2035* is everyone he leaves behind. The people of the Metro were probably condemned to stay down there forever, not because the door was locked, but because they were too afraid to even try it. That image, of a whole world choosing the cage over the unknown, is what makes this the book in the saga I think about the most. If you have any love for bleak, deeply human science fiction, do not stop at *Metro 2033*. The road only gets better, and it ends here.
